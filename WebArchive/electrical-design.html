Section I: Batteries & Drivetrain

We used two 7.2V NiMH batteries to power the robot. The batteries were connected in series to provide a nominal 14.4 V to two buck converters, that supplied 10V to each of two L293 Drivers. These drivers, in turn, provided bidirectional power to the omni-wheels.  A master Teensy LC was used to drive the wheels in the appropriate direction and read in signals from four limit switches; the switches allowed the robot to determine when it had hit a wall and then move its wheels accordingly based on the FSM.

​Drifting is also a major concern for any wall following strategy. In order to make sure the robot was moving straight, the robot was programmed to keep a limit switch high as it was moving. Ex) If the robot was trying to move north along the west wall of the arena, then the North/South wheels would move in the North direction but the East/West wheels would be used to keep the west limit switch high to make sure the robot was consistently pressing along the west wall. In this way, drifting off course was eliminated. 

The limit switches were connected to the teensy on a bread board. Twisted wire pairs were used with molex connectors. A main problem that can occur during testing is the connection from limit switches to the teensy breadboard becoming loose. A temporary fix is to use tape to keep the connections down and have some slack in the wire so the connections are not taut. When pinouts and designs are finalized (which should be done earlier on for more testing time!), then a more permanent fix is to either solder the connections on a protoboard or to hotglue the molex connections to the breadboard. 




​Section II: The Launcher


​We used an L293 Driver to power the flywheel launchers, although unlike the omni wheels, they had a predetermined direction (clockwise for top and counterclockwise for bottom). The wheels on the launcher should only shoot towards the other arena. Another slave teensy, connected to the master teensy, was used to activate the launchers when commanded to by the master teensy in the base.

​The launcher was powered at 12 V and a set PWM was used so that the balls could consistently hit the back of the arena where the dragon was located. Variable PWM was not needed for our strategy although it would have been helpful if we were aiming to target towers at different distances.  Two encoders were made to detect the rpm of the motors. This was done by using a LED/Phototransistor pair (OPB704W) connected to a transresistive amplifier and a comparator stage. The circuit worked perfectly and the rpm of the motors were able to be read, however, due to spurious interrupt events on the launcher teensy we were unable to detect RPM correctly and were unable to debug in time. To adjust the arc of the shot, the launcher angle was physically adjusted although future iterations of this robot could possibly use a stepper motor to adjust the angle of the launcher.
​


Section III: IR Sensing

Our objective with the IR sensing subsystem was to be able to sense the IR signal from a distant target (in this case the dragon) and subsequently activate the launcher. There were five key stages in our IR sensing design:

  • A Transresistive Amplifier with a Phototransistor
  • Highpass (HP) Filter + Voltage Buffer
  • First Amplifier Stage w/ AC Coupling & HP
  • Second Amplifier Stage w/ AC Coupling & HP
  • Comparator



We used the LTR 3208 Phototransistor to sense signals above 1 kHz. Due to noise at DC from sunlight and 60 Hz overhead lights, we employed high pass filters. Two high pass filters were used to have increased attenuation of the signal (-40 dB/dec vs only -20 dB/dec). AC coupling enabled the system to have a DC offset at 2.5V once the high pass filter was applied since high pass filters removed the DC component. We placed the cutoff frequency for the filter at 500 Hz. Two separate gain stages were used rather than a single gain stage in order to have better control over how much gain we could have. Using only a single gain stage also could result in significant noise amplification. 

One interesting pattern we noticed was significant attenuation in the signal coming out of the last gain stage at nighttime compared to daytime due to ambient sunlight. To address this, we added female pin connectors to the second amplification stage and used a 56k Ohm resistor at nighttime and a 27k Ohm resistor during daytime. Since the comparator threshold values were permanently set once the circuit was soldered onto a protoboard, having the ability to change out resistors to have variable gain allowed us to still receive a output from the comparator at different times of the day.

The signal coming from the output of the comparator accurately reflected the frequency and duty cycle of the signal transmitted by the IR beacons. An LED was connected to an output pin of the teensy and was set to high whenever the input signal into the teensy was >1000 kHz. Doing this allowed us to physically tell at what range the IR sensors could detect the IR beacons. The range of the sensing circuit was fairly large such that even LED was high even though it was not directly in front of the beacon. In order to limit the range of the sensor, black straws were put over the phototransistor to limit the angle of detection. The straws were mostly used during hours of bright sunlight, but could still be used during night although shorter straws were prefferable during night hours. 
​
By changing gain and using the straws, the range for which the circuit sensed high could be decreased or increased. We eventually had it so the LED was indicating high for a small range very close to directly in front of the IR beacon. Another slave teensy was used to process IR sensing results (via digital write) and send it to the Master teensy, so it could activate the launcher if a tower beacon or the dragon beacon was sensed.

Section IV: TEENSY Pinouts

Three teensys (one master and two slave teensys) were used to control the flow of inputs and outputs coming from each of the subcomponents. An IR slave teensy was used to receive signal from the output of the comparator and input into the master teensy. The master teensy took the output from the IR slave teensy which stated whether or not the IR beacon was sensed and then controlled the actions of the launcher slave teensy accordingly. The master teensy was also in charge of moving the directions of the wheel on the drive train. 

Specific pinouts will not be stated here for which pin had which input/output. Wiring is up to the user, however, make sure the pins used are capable of digital reading/writing, analog reading/writing, or interrupt service routines. As a side note: when rewiring the circuits, it is easier to keep the pins the same to match the software. Additionally, most of the problems on the electrical side were shorts from circuit components touching each other, loose connections, and most importantly the grounds of the teensys used were not connected together with the grounds of the drivers and motors to create a common. MANY hours could have been saved if we had checked the grounds were connected.

Circuit Debugging Tips

Countless hours were spent debugging the electronic components of our system. Here are some tips/ general debugging guidelines we found to be most helpful.
​
  • CHECK CHECK CHECK your connections. Are they in the right place? Is there a loose connection?
  • GROUND - are all your grounds connected to each other? i.e. Motors driver ground is connected to teensy ground and to common ground. If they aren't then this could be a reason why your component isn't working. 
  • Are there any shorts? Check to see if any of your circuit components are touching each other and creating shorts. When prototyping on a breadboard it is best to seperate your stages so that you can clearly see each stage which makes it easier to check for right/wrong components and connections. It is best to build stage by stage and check to make sure that the signal output of each stage is as desired before adding the next stage.
  • Nothing is coming out of my opamp!? One reason is that the op-amp is fried. One way to check this is to use the opamp as a simple voltage buffer. If you are inputting a voltage and no voltage is being outputted then you have a dead op-amp on hand. Again, check the data sheet to see the maximum input and output supply voltage you can give to the op-amp. Also check to make sure the pinouts of the opamp are correct.
  • DON'T connect your voltage regulator ground to your teensy ground only. This is a sure fire way to fry your teensy because the ground pin of the teensy cannot take the current coming from a 5V voltage regulator. Connect the ground to common ground!
