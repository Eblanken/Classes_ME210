Subsystems

Base

 Because our strategy involved using the walls as guidance, we adopted an omniwheel based design for moving at right angles and bumpers for detecting the walls. By using omniwheels and "hugging" the walls we were able to drive our bot without turning or relying on speed control and dead-reckoning. In addition, springs were added onto the base to keep the limit switches from getting stuck and causing the robot to read a different direction than what it should be.
To prevent our bot from becoming stuck on stray wildfire, we added an armoured skirt built from an extruded aluminum door-threshold mounted on printed supports.

Launcher

To reduce the likelihood that our bot would miss the fast moving dragon and to reduce our reliance on the accuracy of our sensors, we decided to build a multi-barreled launcher with a wide firing arc and a rapid rate of fire powered by 3000 RPM motors. 
Pictured here are supports and encoder wheels for line-detecting reflectivity sensors re-purposed as rotary encoders to allow us to implement speed control for adjusting firing velocity. Unfortunately, though our encoder circuit did work, we received spurious edge detection events and were unable to solve the problem before the final match.

Sensor Tower

 To allow our sensors to see the dragon consistently, we had to support them on a stable platform independent of the adjustable launcher.
